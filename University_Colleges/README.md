# Declining HNC/HND Participation in Scottish Higher Education
## Implications for Lifelong Learning

## Data source
For this project, I analysed selected sheets of two Scottish Government datasets:
- Higher Education Students and Qualifiers (2013–14 to 2022–23):
   - Figure 1 (students),
   - Figure 12 (entrants),
   - Table 24 (qualifiers), including breakdowns by qualification level and institution type (colleges vs HEIs).
- College Statistics Tables (2014–15 to 2023–24):
   - Table 6 (enrolments by subject area) and
   - Table 13 (enrolments by student category).
## Aims
My analysis assesses a potential structural change in Scotland’s lifelong learning (LLL) system, with focusing on:
- Trends in HNC/HND participation
- Differences between college and university provision
- Subject-level shifts in vocational and digital education
- Changes in participation by learner category (age, employment status)

The objective is to determine whether sub-degree lifelong learning pathways are declining, and if yes, who is the most affected.
## Visualisations and Analysis Overview

- Time-series analysis of students, entrants, and qualifiers

  - by institution type - colleges vs other higher education institutes

- **HNC/HND - decrease** in student numbers, entrant numbers, and qualifier number after 2020–21 (~26% in two years)
- Degree - participation remains stable or grows
- Taught Postgraduate (Masters) - continous increase

## Summary Reflection
The data shows widespread declines in subjects traditionally associated with adult upskilling and HND pathways, including: Business, IT, Engineering, Education, Health, and Personal Services. These pathways have historically supported flexible re-entry into education and progression into higher education.

This decline reduces opportunities for stepwise progression in the ‘college → degree → Masters’ model that lifelong learning depends on. While participation has not completely disappeared, the system appears to prioritise initial education and early transitions over adult reskilling and later-life learning. Lifelong learning, in practice, is becoming more linear and less accessible over time.

One of the most surprising findings for me was the 31.6% decline in IT and Information enrolments in colleges from 2014-15 to 2023-24, despite widespread agreement that digital, data, and AI skills will be in very high demand in the future. Colleges, traditionally, are considered to provide accessible routes into digital skills for learners who are excluded from university pathways, including career changers and those returning to education.

This decline suggests a growing mismatch between labour market demand and education provision. Advanced digital skills are increasingly delivered through universities, Masters programmes, or private bootcamps, all of which raise barriers related to cost, prior qualifications, and time. This raises the risk that access to data and AI skills becomes socially stratified with adverse implications for equity and lifelong learning.

The learner category data indicate a pronounced demographic shift. Enrolments among unemployed adults, retired learners, and those in employment have declined considerably, while participation by school-based learners and early intervention cohorts has increased. This pattern suggests that colleges are becoming more tightly integrated into school-to-work pathways, rather than functioning as centres for adult reskilling.

This shift disproportionately affects groups who rely on flexible provision: older learners, women in care-related sectors, lower-income adults, and those displaced by automation. Lifelong learning participation is becoming younger, more full-time, and less compatible with caring responsibilities or employment.
